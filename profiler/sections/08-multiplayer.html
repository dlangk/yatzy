Playing Against Someone Else

Everything so far has been single-player optimization. But Yatzy is usually played against others. Does the optimal single-player strategy also win the most games head-to-head?

We simulated one million two-player games for each value of θ, always pitting the θ-player against an EV-optimal (θ=0) opponent. The results:

Win Rate vs an EV-Optimal Opponent

Each point represents 1 million games between a θ-player and an EV-optimal opponent. Color indicates risk attitude (blue = risk-averse, red = risk-seeking). Hover for exact stats.

The EV-optimal strategy (θ=0) is also approximately win-rate-optimal. No value of θ consistently beats it head-to-head. Risk-averse strategies (negative θ) lose around 49–50% of games — close, but still losing. Risk-seeking strategies lose badly, dropping below 40% win rate at θ=0.15 and below 35% at θ=0.2.

The asymmetry from the single-player analysis carries over: risk-aversion costs you very little in win rate (a percent or two), while risk-seeking drops your win rate dramatically. If you're playing competitively, the EV-optimal strategy is the right choice, but a mildly risk-averse strategy is nearly as good.
