The Distribution Is Not What You Think

When I first simulated a million games, I expected a nice bell curve. What I got was something much more interesting. Drag the slider below to see how the score distribution changes as you adjust the risk parameter (more on that parameter shortly).

0 (EV-optimal)

The dashed line shows the θ=0 (EV-optimal) distribution for reference. Drag the slider to see how different risk attitudes reshape the distribution.

The distribution is clearly not Gaussian. There are visible bumps and a characteristic right shoulder. The explanation turns out to be beautifully simple: the score distribution is a mixture of four sub-populations, created by two binary events that dominate the variance.

The first event is the upper section bonus: either you reach 63 points and earn the 50-point bonus (87% of the time at θ=0), or you don't. The second is Yatzy: either you roll five of a kind at some point (39% of the time), or you don't. These two events are roughly independent, and each shifts your score by about 50 points, creating four clusters.

Score Distribution as a Four-Component Mixture

Each component is a sub-population defined by whether the player hit the upper bonus and/or scored a Yatzy. Click "Build up" to see each component added one by one. Click any legend item to toggle it.

The "Bonus + Yatzy" population (34.7% of games, mean 288) forms the right shoulder. The "No bonus, no Yatzy" population (6.1%, mean 164) creates the thin left tail. The two middle populations overlap to produce the apparent main peak. This isn't a statistical curiosity — it directly informs strategy. Any play that risks the upper bonus is effectively gambling 50 points.
